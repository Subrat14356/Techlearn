Generations of Computers

1. First Generation (1940-1956)

First generation computers used vacuum tubes for circuitry and magnetic drums for memory.

Characteristics of Vacuum Tube Computers:

  • Occupied a lot of space
  • Very expensive to operate
  • Consumed large amounts of electricity and generated significant heat
  • Relied on machine language
  • Could solve only one problem at a time

ENIAC

Electronic Numerical Integrator And Computer

Developed by John W. Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert Jr. at the University of Pennsylvania.

EDVAC

Electronic Discrete Variable Automatic Computer

Developed by J. Presper Eckert Jr. and John W. Mauchly.

It was the first stored-program computer.

UNIVAC-1

Universal Automatic Computer

Developed by J. Presper Eckert Jr. and John W. Mauchly.

First commercial computer delivered to a business client (the U.S. Census Bureau).

2. Second Generation (1956-1963)

Second generation computers replaced vacuum tubes with transistors.

Characteristics:

  • Used transistors which were more reliable
  • Smaller in size compared to first generation
  • Consumed less electricity and generated less heat
  • Faster than first generation computers
  • Used magnetic core technology for primary memory

Examples of Second Generation Computers:

  • UNIVAC-III
  • IBM 700 series
  • IBM 1401
  • NCR 300 series

3. Third Generation (1964-1971)

Third generation computers used integrated circuits (ICs) instead of individual transistors.

Characteristics:

  • Used integrated circuits (chips)
  • Smaller, faster, and more reliable than second generation
  • First time users interacted with computers using keyboards and monitors
  • Used operating systems that allowed multiple applications to run simultaneously
  • Development of minicomputers

4. Fourth Generation (1971-Present)

Fourth generation computers used microprocessors with VLSI (Very Large Scale Integration) technology.

Characteristics:

  • Use of microprocessors (thousands of ICs on a single chip)
  • Development of personal computers
  • Development of GUIs, mouse, and handheld devices
  • Computers became small enough to fit on a desk or in a lap
  • Development of computer networks and the Internet

Technologies:

VLSI - Very Large Scale Integration

ULSI - Ultra Large Scale Integration

Examples:

  • Desktop computers
  • Laptop computers
  • Development of the World Wide Web (WWW)

5. Fifth Generation (Present and Beyond)

Fifth generation computers are based on artificial intelligence and parallel processing.

Characteristics:

  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) capabilities
  • Voice recognition and natural language processing
  • Quantum computing and nanotechnology
  • Ability to learn and self-organize
  • Can respond to natural language input

Examples:

  • Laptops and tablets
  • LCD and LED display devices
  • AI assistants (Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant)
  • Robotics and expert systems
