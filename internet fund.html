Introduction to the Internet & Database Constraints

Constraints in Databases

Constraints are rules used to enforce data integrity in a database. Common constraints include:

Primary Key Constraint

Ensures that each record has a unique identifier and cannot contain null values.

Foreign Key Constraint

Maintains referential integrity between tables by enforcing relationships between data in different tables.

Check Constraint

Ensures data meets a specific condition or set of conditions before insertion or update.

Default Constraint

Assigns a default value to a column when no value is explicitly provided during insertion.

What is the Internet?

The Internet is a worldwide network of interconnected computers that communicate using standardized protocols. It consists of multiple small networks connected using networking devices and functions as a single global system.

It acts as a window to global information and is often referred to as the "Information Superhighway." The Internet is also known as Interwork or Internetwork, emphasizing its nature as a network of networks.

History of the Internet

1962
J.C.R. Licklider of MIT proposed the concept of a "Galactic Network" for data sharing between computers, which became the foundational idea for the Internet.
Packet Switching
This revolutionary technology, developed by Paul Baran and Donald Davies, became the foundation of today's Internet by allowing efficient data transmission across networks.

Key Internet Milestones

1968
The first network of computers was installed at the National Research Laboratory of England, marking early experiments in computer networking.
1969
ARPANET was established by the Pentagon's Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA), connecting four university computers. This network (originally called IMP) became the precursor to the modern Internet.
1972
ARPANET had expanded to contain 23 nodes, demonstrating the growing potential of computer networking.
Early 1970s
Raymond Samuel Tomlinson wrote the first email program and introduced the now-ubiquitous @ symbol to separate usernames from host names in email addresses.
1973
The first international node of ARPANET was set up between England and Norway, beginning the global expansion of the network.
1974
Bob Kahn and Vint Cerf published their seminal paper on Transmission Control Protocol (TCP), laying the foundation for modern Internet protocols.
1983
TCP/IP became the standard protocol for ARPANET, marking the official birth of the Internet as we know it today.
1989
Tim Berners-Lee proposed the World Wide Web while working at CERN, creating the system that would make the Internet accessible to the general public.
